Introduction to Light

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation that enables us to see objects. It is a transverse wave that travels in straight lines and has a finite speed of approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s in vacuum.

Key Point: Light exhibits both wave and particle nature (wave-particle duality). It can be reflected, refracted, and diffracted.

Reflection of Light

Reflection is the phenomenon in which light bounces off a surface. The law of reflection states:

  • Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection
  • The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal all lie in the same plane
Law of Reflection:
∠i = ∠r
where i = angle of incidence, r = angle of reflection

Types of Reflection

  • Regular Reflection: Occurs on smooth, polished surfaces. Parallel rays remain parallel after reflection.
  • Diffuse Reflection: Occurs on rough, uneven surfaces. Parallel rays are reflected in different directions.
Example: A mirror produces regular reflection, while a wall produces diffuse reflection.

Spherical Mirrors

Mirrors that have curved surfaces are called spherical mirrors. They are of two types:

  • Concave Mirror: Converging mirror - reflects light towards a point
  • Convex Mirror: Diverging mirror - reflects light away from a point
Note: This chapter covers basic principles. For detailed formulas and ray diagrams, refer to NCERT textbook.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Light travels in straight lines
  • ✓ Reflection follows the law of incidence = reflection
  • ✓ Two types of reflection: Regular and Diffuse
  • ✓ Spherical mirrors can be concave or convex
