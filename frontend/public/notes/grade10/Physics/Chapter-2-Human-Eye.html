Structure of the Human Eye

The human eye is a complex organ that allows us to see objects around us. Light enters the eye and is focused on the retina, which sends signals to the brain.

Key Point: The eye acts like a camera with the cornea and lens focusing light, and the retina acting as the light-sensitive film.

Parts of the Human Eye

  • Cornea: Transparent front layer that focuses light
  • Iris: Colored part that controls pupil size
  • Pupil: Opening through which light enters
  • Lens: Focuses light onto the retina
  • Retina: Contains light-sensitive cells (rods and cones)
  • Optic Nerve: Sends signals to the brain

Defects of Vision

Common vision problems can be corrected using lenses:

  • Myopia (Short-sightedness): Can see nearby objects clearly but distant objects appear blurred. Corrected using concave lens.
  • Hypermetropia (Long-sightedness): Can see distant objects clearly but nearby objects appear blurred. Corrected using convex lens.
  • Presbyopia: Age-related loss of eye focusing ability.
Example: A myopic person cannot see the whiteboard clearly in a classroom and requires spectacles with concave lenses.

Dispersion of Light

Dispersion is the phenomenon in which white light is split into its component colors. The different colors have different wavelengths and travel at slightly different speeds in a medium.

Rainbow Formation:
White light → Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Indigo, Violet
(ROYGBIV - Order of colors in rainbow)

Scattering of Light

Scattering is the phenomenon in which light is reflected in many directions when it strikes a medium with suspended particles.

  • Blue light has shorter wavelength and scatters more (blue sky)
  • Red light has longer wavelength and scatters less (red sunset)

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Human eye acts like a camera
  • ✓ Myopia corrected with concave lens
  • ✓ Hypermetropia corrected with convex lens
  • ✓ White light disperses into 7 colors
  • ✓ Scattering depends on wavelength
